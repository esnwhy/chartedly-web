# Persona 02: Yuki Tanaka

## Demographics
- **Name:** Yuki Tanaka (田中 ゆき)
- **Age:** 22
- **Location:** Kichijoji, Tokyo
- **Nationality:** Japanese
- **Occupation:** University student (3rd year, marketing major) + part-time konbini staff
- **Income:** ~¥80,000/month (part-time + family allowance)
- **Language:** Japanese (native), English (basic — can read simple product names and UI labels)
- **Relationship status:** Single, lives with parents

## Tech Savviness
- **Level:** Very high (digital native)
- **Primary device:** iPhone 14, no personal laptop (uses university computer lab)
- **Daily apps:** Instagram, TikTok, LINE, @cosme, LIPS (beauty app), X (Twitter), Amazon JP, ZOZOTOWN
- **Browser behavior:** Primarily uses apps over mobile browser; discovers products through social feeds, not search engines
- **Comfort with new platforms:** Extremely high for apps with strong visual design; low patience for text-heavy or dated-looking interfaces

## Primary Goals
1. **Stay current on trending beauty and lifestyle products** — she wants to know what's popular this week, not this year, and see it presented visually with real user photos
2. **Compare products side-by-side before spending** — as a price-sensitive student, she needs to justify every purchase by confirming it's the best value for her specific needs (skin type, budget, aesthetic preference)
3. **Discover new product categories beyond beauty** — she's increasingly interested in tech accessories, stationery, and home goods but doesn't have a trusted go-to source outside of @cosme for those categories

## Pain Points
1. **@cosme is beauty-only and feels dated:** She relies heavily on @cosme for skincare/makeup rankings but the interface feels cluttered compared to Instagram. She wishes product discovery felt more like scrolling a curated visual feed. For non-beauty categories, she has no equivalent trusted platform. *Synthesized from @cosme user reviews on Google Play and Japanese UX design research (btrax, designmadeinjapan.com).*
2. **Information overload without curation:** Between Instagram ads, TikTok recommendations, @cosme rankings, and friend opinions, she encounters dozens of product suggestions daily but has no way to filter signal from noise. She often experiences decision paralysis. *Synthesized from Gen Z browsing behavior research (PwC, HubSpot, Bazaarvoice) and Japanese consumer behavior studies (Kadence, Rakuten).*
3. **Distrust of paid influencer content:** She's increasingly skeptical of sponsored posts on Instagram and TikTok — she can tell when an influencer is being paid, and it erodes her trust. She prefers platforms where rankings are based on aggregated real user data. *Synthesized from Gen Z consumer trust research (SurveyMonkey, eMarketer) and @cosme trust model analysis.*

## Jobs-To-Be-Done Statement
> **When** I see a trending product on social media or hear about it from friends, **I want to** quickly verify if it's actually good by checking aggregated rankings and real user reviews in a visually engaging format, **so I can** feel confident spending my limited budget on something that truly works for me.

## Scenario Narrative
Yuki is on the Chuo Line heading to university. She opens TikTok and sees a creator raving about a new lip tint from rom&nd. It looks amazing in the video, but she's been burned before — she bought a hyped concealer last month that didn't match her skin tone at all. She switches to @cosme and searches the product. It has 4.2 stars with 87 reviews, but the reviews are mostly text and she has to scroll through pages of them to get a feel.

She thinks, "I wish I could just see this ranked against the other top lip tints, with photos, in one glance." She opens Instagram and searches the product hashtag — she finds beautiful photos but zero objective comparison. She saves the post and tells herself she'll "research it later," but later never comes. The product sits in her mental wishlist for weeks.

**If Chartedly existed:** Yuki sees the TikTok, opens Chartedly, and taps "Lip Tints." She sees a visually rich grid — almost like Netflix browsing but for products. The rom&nd tint is ranked #3. She taps it and instantly sees how it compares to #1 and #2 on color payoff, staying power, and price. User-submitted photos show it on different skin tones. She decides to buy #1 instead — better value, better reviews — and does so in under 2 minutes.
