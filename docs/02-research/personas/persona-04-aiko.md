# Persona 04: Aiko Yamamoto

## Demographics
- **Name:** Aiko Yamamoto (山本 あいこ)
- **Age:** 40
- **Location:** Musashino, Tokyo
- **Nationality:** Japanese
- **Occupation:** Administrative manager at a mid-size trading company
- **Income:** ~¥5.5M/year (household income ~¥10M with spouse)
- **Language:** Japanese (native), English (limited — passed Eiken Grade 2 in university, rarely uses it)
- **Relationship status:** Married, two children (ages 7 and 4)

## Tech Savviness
- **Level:** Moderate
- **Primary device:** iPhone 13 (95% of personal internet usage), family iPad, work-issued Windows laptop
- **Daily apps:** LINE, Yahoo! Japan, Amazon JP, Rakuten, Instagram (mostly browsing, not posting), cookpad
- **Browser behavior:** Searches on Google JP or Yahoo JP; strongly prefers mobile-optimized sites. Abandons any page that requires horizontal scrolling or complex navigation
- **Comfort with new platforms:** Low to moderate; will adopt a new app only if a trusted friend recommends it or if it clearly saves her time on the first visit

## Primary Goals
1. **Find the best household products quickly** — she needs reliable answers to "which robot vacuum should I buy?" or "which laundry detergent is best for kids' sensitive skin?" and she needs them in under 5 minutes, not 50
2. **Trust the ranking methodology** — she's been burned by Amazon reviews that turned out to be fake and by rakuten recommendations that were clearly pay-to-play. She values transparency about how products are evaluated
3. **Reduce decision fatigue** — she makes hundreds of micro-decisions daily (work, childcare, household). For product purchases, she wants a clear "#1 pick" with a brief explanation of why, not an open-ended comparison

## Pain Points
1. **Time poverty kills thorough research:** Between her full-time job, school pickups, dinner prep, and bedtime routines, she has maybe 15 minutes of discretionary screen time before falling asleep. She cannot afford the 30-60 minute research sessions that platforms like kakaku.com or mybest seem to require. *Synthesized from Japanese working mother time-use surveys and consumer behavior research (McKinsey, Kadence).*
2. **Existing platforms are information-dense but not decision-efficient:** kakaku.com has comprehensive data but feels overwhelming. mybest articles are thorough but long. Amazon JP sorts by "best sellers" but she's learned that best-selling doesn't mean best-quality. None of them give her a confident answer quickly. *Synthesized from kakaku.com and mybest platform analyses and Japanese UX design research (btrax, McLaren Group).*
3. **Distrust of online reviews is growing:** She read a news segment about fake Amazon reviews and organized review manipulation (sakura reviews/サクラレビュー). Now she second-guesses everything. She'd rather ask her mama-tomo (mom friends) in LINE groups, but they don't always have experience with the product she needs. *Synthesized from Japanese consumer trust research (Rakuten marketplace expectations, igni7e.com trusted review platforms, Santander consumer profile).*

> **When** I need to purchase a household or family product and have very limited time to research, **I want to** see a clear, trustworthy top recommendation with a transparent rationale in under 3 minutes, **so I can** make a confident buying decision without anxiety or wasted time.

## Scenario Narrative
It's 10:15 PM. The kids are finally asleep. Aiko's husband is watching TV. She has maybe 20 minutes before she falls asleep herself. She picks up her iPhone — their air purifier broke this morning and she needs to order a replacement before the weekend.

She opens Amazon JP and searches "空気清浄機" (air purifier). There are 4,000+ results. She sorts by reviews — the top result has 4.5 stars but 30% of the reviews look suspicious (generic praise, posted on the same day). She opens kakaku.com — it shows 380 models with specs she doesn't fully understand (CADR, HEPA grade, ionizer vs. filter). She starts reading a mybest article titled "おすすめの空気清浄機ランキング" — it's 8,000 words long with 15 products reviewed. She gets to product #4 before her eyes start closing.

She gives up, asks her husband "ダイキンでいいかな？" (Is Daikin okay?), he shrugs, and she orders the first Daikin model on Amazon. She's not confident it was the best choice, but she's too tired to care.

**If Chartedly existed:** Aiko opens Chartedly, taps "Home Appliances > Air Purifiers." The top pick is displayed prominently with a clear "Why #1" summary: best CADR for rooms under 20㎡, quiet mode tested at 22dB, best value under ¥30,000. She sees it's a Daikin model — good, she trusts the brand — and taps "Buy on Amazon JP." Total time: 2 minutes. She falls asleep confident she made the right choice.

## Empathy Map

### Thinks
- "I don't have time for this — just tell me the answer"
- "Are these reviews real? I read that article about sakura reviews..."
- "kakaku.com has good data but I feel like I need an engineering degree to use it"
- "My mom friends recommended this brand but is it actually the best?"

### Feels
- Chronically time-pressured — every minute of personal time is precious
- Anxious about making the wrong purchase because returns are hassle in Japan
- Relieved when a trusted source gives a clear recommendation
- Mildly guilty for defaulting to "whatever Amazon recommends" when she's too tired to research

### Says
- "ママ友に聞いてみる" (I'll ask my mom friends)
- "レビュー、信用できるかな？" (Can I trust these reviews?)
- "もう疲れた、これでいいや" (I'm tired, this one is fine)
- "比較サイト見たけど、多すぎて分からない" (I looked at a comparison site but there are too many, I can't tell)

### Does
- Asks mama-tomo LINE groups for product recommendations before searching online
- Defaults to familiar brands (Daikin, Panasonic, Shiseido) when uncertain
- Uses Amazon JP "best seller" rankings as a heuristic despite knowing they're imperfect
- Reads only the first 3-5 reviews on any product before deciding
- Shops during commute on smartphone — never opens laptop for personal product research
- Trusts mybest when she has time to read their articles, but rarely finishes them
- Prioritizes products with easy return policies or available at nearby stores
