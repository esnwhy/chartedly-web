# Chartedly Tone of Voice Guide

**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Role:** INK -- Senior Content Strategist & Copywriter
**Purpose:** Define the voice, personality, and language standards for all Chartedly content in English and Japanese

---

## 1. Brand Personality Definition

Chartedly sounds like a 30-year-old, design-literate friend who has lived in Japan for four years. They are the person in the group chat everyone asks before buying anything -- not because they obsess over shopping, but because they cut through noise and say, "Just get this one. Here's why."

### Personality Traits (Ranked)

| Rank | Trait | Expression |
|------|-------|------------|
| 1 | Clear-headed | Lead with the answer. No throat-clearing paragraphs. |
| 2 | Helpful | Every sentence exists to solve a problem or prevent one. |
| 3 | Design-conscious | Care about presentation. Well-structured pages signal reliability. |
| 4 | Gently witty | Dry humor from shared experience, never forced. |
| 5 | Confident | "Our top pick" not "you might want to consider." |
| 6 | Culturally aware | Respect Japan. Laugh at shared confusion, never at the culture. |

### What Chartedly Is NOT

- Not a hype machine ("OMG you NEED this!!!")
- Not a textbook ("The epidermis requires protection from ultraviolet radiation")
- Not a content mill ("In this article, we will discuss the top 10 best...")
- Not sarcastic or cynical about Japan
- Not overly casual or slang-heavy ("this stuff slaps fr fr")

## 2. English Voice

### Register: Smart Casual (4/10 Formality)

More polished than a Reddit post. Less stiff than a newspaper article. Think: a well-edited personal blog written by someone who respects your time.

### Vocabulary Rules

- Use "buy" not "purchase." "Works well" not "delivers exceptional performance."
- Contractions are standard: "it's," "we've," "don't," "you'll."
- First-person plural for editorial voice: "we tested," "our top pick."
- Second-person for advice: "you'll want," "if you have dry skin."
- Technical terms are fine when they are the clearest option (SPF 50+, retinol, HEPA filter) but define on first use.
- Japanese product names appear in original form with a brief English description: "Biore UV Aqua Rich Watery Essence (a lightweight, watery sunscreen)."

### Sentence Structure

- Short to medium sentences. Average length: 12-18 words.
- Active voice by default. Passive only when the actor is irrelevant.
- Lead with useful information: "This sunscreen absorbs quickly and leaves no white cast" not "After extensive testing across multiple application scenarios, we found that absorption rates were notably superior."
- Bullet points and structured lists are encouraged, but embedded in narrative flow. Pages should not read like spec sheets.

### Humor: Moderate-Low (3/10)

- Dry, observational humor from shared experience is welcome.
- Puns, memes, and forced jokes are not.
- Good: "You know that moment when you accidentally buy fabric softener instead of laundry detergent because the bottles are identical? We made this guide so that never happens again."
- Bad: "Konnichiwa, sunscreen lovers! It's SPF o'clock!"
- Never mock Japanese products, culture, or language. The humor comes from the universal confusion of navigating an unfamiliar system, not from the system being wrong.

### Reading Level

- Target: Flesch Reading Ease score of 65 or higher.
- This means roughly 8th-grade reading level -- clear to non-native English speakers, scannable for busy readers.
- No sentences over 30 words. Break them up.
- Avoid nominalization: "We recommend" not "Our recommendation is."

### Punctuation Preferences

- Oxford comma: always. ("Sunscreens, moisturizers, and cleansers.")
- Em dashes for asides -- like this -- used sparingly.
- Semicolons: avoid. Use two short sentences instead.
- Exclamation marks: extremely rare. Only in genuinely surprising contexts.
- Ellipses: never in body copy.
- No emoji in body text or headings. Use in social media captions only.

## 3. Japanese Voice

### Register: Polite but Not Overly Formal

- **Reviews and editorial content:** だ/である調 (plain assertive form). This conveys authority and directness, matching the confident English voice.
- **UI elements, navigation, microcopy:** です/ます調 (polite form). This feels natural for interface text and shows appropriate courtesy.
- **Legal pages:** です/ます調 with formal vocabulary. Standard for Japanese legal and compliance text.

### Key Japanese Voice Rules

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Use 漢語 (Sino-Japanese words) for precision | 「比較」not 「くらべること」 |
| Use 和語 (native words) for warmth | 「選ぶ」not 「選択する」 for casual contexts |
| Keep sentences shorter than typical Japanese web copy | Max 60 characters per sentence in body text |
| Avoid excessive keigo | 「ご覧ください」is fine; 「ご高覧賜りますようお願い申し上げます」is not |
| Use furigana for uncommon kanji in educational contexts | 日焼け止め（ひやけどめ）on first mention in guides |
| Katakana for foreign brand names | ビオレ, アネッサ, スキンアクア |
| Natural sentence endings | 「だ。」「である。」「だろう。」for reviews |

### Product Name Conventions

Always display Japanese product names in the following order:
1. English brand name + product name (primary display)
2. Japanese name in katakana/kanji (in parentheses or subtitle)

Example in English context: Biore UV Aqua Rich Watery Essence (ビオレUV アクアリッチ ウォータリーエッセンス)
Example in Japanese context: ビオレUV アクアリッチ ウォータリーエッセンス / Biore UV Aqua Rich Watery Essence

## 4. Tone Variations by Context

| Context | EN Tone | JP Tone | Key Principle |
|---------|---------|---------|---------------|
| Homepage | Confident, inviting, concise | 親しみやすく、簡潔に | Clarity of purpose -- what is this site? |
| Comparison articles | Authoritative but approachable | 信頼感があり、わかりやすい | Data-forward with human judgment |
| Category pages | Organized, scannable, efficient | 整理されていて見やすい | Let structure speak; minimal personality |
| About / Methodology | Sincere, direct, no marketing fluff | 誠実で率直に | Explain the mission honestly |
| Error pages (404) | Lightly humorous, helpful | 軽いユーモアと親切さ | Acknowledge the inconvenience without being cutesy |
| Social media | Most casual, punchy | カジュアルで簡潔 | Visual-first, can use fragments |
| Legal pages | Clear, straightforward | 正確で丁寧 | Compliance without legalese overkill |

## 5. "We Say / We Don't Say" Table

| # | We Say | We Don't Say | Why |
|---|--------|-------------|-----|
| 1 | "Our top pick" | "The number one best product ever" | Confident, not hyperbolic |
| 2 | "We tested" | "We had the opportunity to evaluate" | Plain language |
| 3 | "Buy on Amazon JP" | "Click here to purchase this product" | Direct, specific, actionable |
| 4 | "Works well under makeup" | "Demonstrates exceptional performance as a cosmetic base" | Human language |
| 5 | "This one is not worth the price" | "This product underperforms relative to its price point" | Honest and blunt |
| 6 | "Best for dry skin" | "Ideal for individuals experiencing cutaneous dehydration" | Accessible |
| 7 | "Skip this one" | "We would not necessarily recommend this option" | Decisive |
| 8 | "Costs around 900 yen" | "Priced at approximately 900 JPY" | Conversational |
| 9 | "Available at most drugstores" | "Can be procured at a wide variety of pharmaceutical retail establishments" | Human-scale language |
| 10 | "Updated March 2026" | "This article was last revised on the date indicated above" | Clear and specific |
| 11 | "You'll notice a light citrus scent" | "The olfactory profile features citrus notes" | Sensory, not clinical |
| 12 | "Smooth, non-sticky finish" | "The tactile sensation post-application is notably non-adhesive" | Felt experience |
| 13 | "We compared 8 sunscreens" | "In this comprehensive analysis, we examined 8 sunscreen products" | Get to it |
| 14 | "Here's what to know" | "The following information may be of relevance" | Friendly signpost |
| 15 | "SPF 50+ (the highest rating in Japan)" | "SPF 50+, which represents the maximum Sun Protection Factor classification within the Japanese regulatory framework" | Informative without lecturing |
| 16 | "Great for beginners" | "Suitable for those who are new to Japanese skincare products" | Short and warm |
| 17 | "This absorbs in seconds" | "Rapid absorption is a distinguishing characteristic" | Show, don't tell |
| 18 | "We buy everything ourselves" | "All products are independently procured by our editorial team" | Builds trust naturally |
| 19 | "No white cast" | "Does not exhibit visible residue upon application" | The way people actually talk |
| 20 | "Found at Don Quijote and Matsumoto Kiyoshi" | "Available at select retail partners including but not limited to..." | Specific is useful |

## 7. Numbers, Dates, and Formatting Conventions

| Element | English | Japanese |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Currency | 900 yen or 900 yen ($6) | 900円 |
| Dates | March 2026 or March 15, 2026 | 2026年3月 or 2026年3月15日 |
| Scores | 9.2/10 | 9.2/10 |
| Product counts | "8 products compared" | 「8製品を比較」 |
| Percentages | 30% | 30% |
| Lists | Oxford comma, periods at end of full sentences | 読点（、）で区切り、句点（。）で終了 |
| Emphasis | **Bold** for key phrases. No ALL CAPS. | **太字**で強調。全角感嘆符は控えめに |

## 8. Voice Checklist (For Every Piece of Content)

Before publishing, verify:

- [ ] Does the opening sentence deliver useful information? (No "In this article, we will...")
- [ ] Is every sentence under 25 words?
- [ ] Would a non-native English speaker understand this without a dictionary?
- [ ] Are Japanese product names displayed with both English and Japanese versions?
- [ ] Is the tone confident without being arrogant?
- [ ] Does the humor (if any) come from shared experience, not mockery?
- [ ] Are affiliate links clearly disclosed?
- [ ] Is the Japanese version naturally written (not machine-translated)?
- [ ] Does the page feel complete and intentional, not thin or padded?
- [ ] Would you send this page to a friend who just moved to Japan?
